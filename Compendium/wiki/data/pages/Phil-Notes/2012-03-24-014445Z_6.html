“And truly, I saw something the like of which I had never seen before. A young shepherd I saw; writhing, choking, twitching, his face distorted, with a thick back snake hanging from his mouth.” (p. 127)

I believe this passage is a riddle about his soul’s third metamorphsis. It starts with him talking to his burden the gravity who is a dwarf. I see the dwarf as a reference to the self that is not tied to the ego. That is why he tells the dwarf you or I dwarf and I is stronger. Gravity is a constant pull on humanity. This dwarf represents the constant pull of impulse on himself. It might be easier to just call the dwarf the id. I don’t think that the use of a dwarf can be over looked though. In hindu tradition Shiva dances upon the dwarf of ignorance. This is a powerful symbol that works here as well. The dwarf which is our impulses is ignorant of reality. It constantly works to contain the ego. It constantly fights to keep the ego from climbing above itself, because the ego has the ability to understand the universe and the fact that it is something to be overcome or it will think it is everything. If the ego grows it is because of the pull of the id and then the person becomes attached to the world and to himself. After discussing with the dwarf for a bit, a dog howls. This for me is a call to the hero. It invokes the idea of child which Nietzsche names as the third metamorphosis of the soul. After this Zarathustra see’s a shepherd (I believe that he is this shepherd. He uses this analogy because jesus was a shepherd and because the other two parts were about him trying to lead the people. He has been acting like the shepherd) choking on a black snake (The snake is a metaphor that Zarathustra creates in the very beginning when he talks about his animal friends the eagle and the snake. The snake is wisdom. So I believe that the shepherd is in fact chocking on his own wisdom.) I may have read something like this in the intro. I’m sure its why I seem to be having such great success at understanding all of this book. Even still I believe this understanding to be interesting. I will definitely spend more time mulling this over.

“–Meanwhile the shepherd bit down as my shout advised him; he bit with a good bite! Far away he spat the head of the snake – and he leaped to his feet. –

No longer shepherd, no longer human – a transformed, illuminated, laughing being!" (p. 127)

By ridding himself of the wisdom on which he was choking. The shepherd soul transformed into the child’s. He realized he was not wise. The search for wisdom was not in fact the end all. It was something which made him proud. It was the desert in which the Lion had claimed. In order to overcome the lion, he had to spit out the wisdom he was choking on.

“ One has to learn to love oneself – thus I teach – with a hale and healthy love, so that one can stand oneself and not have to roam around.

Such roaming around christens itself "love of the neighbor": these words so far have produced the best lying and hypocrisy, and especially from those whom all the world found heavy.
And truly, this is not a command for today or tomorrow, this learning to love oneself. Instead, of all arts this is the most subtle, cunning, ultimate and most patient.
For one's own, you see, all one's own is well hidden; and of all buried treasures, one's own is the latest to be dug up – this is the spirit of gravity's doing.
Almost from the cradle, grave words and values are imparted to us; "good" and "evil" this dowry calls itself. For its sake we are forgiven for being alive.
And for this reason one lets the little children come to one, in order to restrain them early on from loving themselves; this is the spirit of gravity's doing.
And we – we faithfully lug what is imparted to us on hard shoulders and over rough mountains! And if we sweat, then we are told: "Yes, life is a heavy burden!"
But only the human being is a heavy burden to himself! This is because he lugs too much that is foreign to him. Like a camel he kneels down and allows himself to be well burdened." (p.154)
